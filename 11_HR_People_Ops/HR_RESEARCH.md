## 2. Employee vs independent contractor — the classification question

Kenya's **Employment Act 2007** defines an employee as a person employed for wages or salary.
The law distinguishes:

- **Contract *of* service** → **employee**. Creates the full employment relationship and its
  statutory rights.
- **Contract *for* service** → **independent contractor**. Self-employed, working on their own
  terms.

### 2.1 The three tests Kenyan courts apply

| Test | Question | Why it matters to Arika |
|---|---|---|
| **Control** | Who decides *what* work, *how*, and *when*? | A freelancer told exactly what to shoot, how, and when looks like an employee |
| **Integration** | Is the work **integral to the business**, or ancillary? | 🔴 **The dangerous one — see below** |
| **Substance over form** | What is the *reality*, regardless of the contract's label? | **Calling someone a contractor does not make them one** |

**Substance over form is settled by case law.** In *Maurice Oduor Okech v Chequered Flag
Limited* (2013), the court held it must go *"beyond mere terminologies employed by the parties
and inquire into the entire spectrum of facts and circumstances."*

### 2.2 🔴 The Integration Test is Arika's specific risk

The owner's stated intent is to engage **freelance photographers for marketing** and other
short-term contract work (2026-07-15).

**But Arika sells creative production.** Design (19) is a delivery department with its own
Production Engine; Marketing (03) and Content (04) are core revenue functions. **A photographer
producing assets that Arika sells is doing work that is arguably integral to the business, not
ancillary to it.**

Compare: a plumber fixing the office sink is plainly ancillary. A photographer producing the
imagery inside a client deliverable is much closer to the core.

**This does not mean it cannot be done.** It means the arrangement must be **structured to
survive the tests** — genuine autonomy over method, project-scoped rather than continuous,
their own equipment, freedom to work for others, no integration into internal process — and
**counsel must confirm the structure**, not the label.

### 2.3 What misclassification actually costs

If a contractor is reclassified as an employee by a court or the **KRA**:
- **Back wages and benefits** — notice pay, leave, sick leave, **severance**
- Unremitted statutory deductions, plus penalties and interest
- **Kenyan labour inspectors and the KRA can audit arrangements they suspect are
  misclassified** — this is not only a risk if someone sues
- Reputational damage

**→ Counsel question A:** *Given Arika sells creative and marketing services, can a freelance
photographer/designer be engaged as a genuine independent contractor, and what structure makes
that defensible under the Control and Integration tests?*

## 3. Real payroll mechanics — what an employee actually costs

**These rates are real and current for 2026. Re-verify before acting; they move annually.**

| Item | Employee pays | **Employer pays** | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PAYE** | 10% on first KES 24,000/mo → **35%** above KES 800,000/mo | — (withhold + remit) | Personal relief **KES 2,400/mo**. Calculated *after* NSSF/SHIF/Housing are deducted |
| **NSSF** | 3% | **3%** | 6% combined, **capped KES 12,960/mo** combined. Upper pensionable limit **KES 72,000** |
| **SHIF** | **2.75%** of gross, min KES 300/mo, **no cap** | **1.375%** | SHIF replaced NHIF (late 2024). Employee-only deduction — **but the employer pays 1.375%, a cost that did not exist under NHIF** |
| **Housing Levy** | 1.5% | **1.5%** | 3% combined |

### 3.1 The number that matters

> **The employer-matched portions add roughly 7.5% on top of gross pay**, before any provider
> margin.

So a **KES 100,000/month** salary costs Arika roughly **KES 107,500/month** — plus the
administrative duty to compute, withhold, and remit correctly.

### 3.2 The obligations nobody costs in

- **Remittance by the 9th of the following month.** Every month. **Late remittance is the
  employer's liability, not the employee's.**
- **Annual PAYE returns by 30 June.**
- **The employer carries the legal duty** to deduct, match, and remit on time.

**For a solo operator this is the real cost of the first hire — not the salary, the
machinery.** It is a recurring, dated, personally-liable obligation that did not exist the day
before. **This is the strongest single argument for engaging an accountant before, or at the
same time as, the first employee** — which is exactly what the owner has already identified
(`PEOPLE_DOCTRINE.md` §5).

### 3.3 Contractor cost, by contrast

A genuine independent contractor invoices; there is **no PAYE, NSSF, SHIF, or Housing Levy
obligation on Arika**, and no 7.5% add-on.

> **⚠️ That gap is exactly why misclassification is policed.** The cost saving *is* the
> motive the KRA looks for. **A contractor arrangement chosen because it is cheaper, rather
> than because it is genuinely a contract *for* service, is the fact pattern that loses.**

**→ Accountant question A:** *Confirm the 2026 rates, the withholding-tax position on
contractor payments, and what Arika must do before its first hire or first contractor payment.*

## 4. Remote work

The owner works remotely and intends any future people to as well.

**Not researched, deliberately — and both are real:**
- **Kenyan law on remote employees** — obligations do not disappear because nobody comes to an
  office. Written contracts, statutory deductions, and working-time rules still apply.
- **Cross-border engagement.** If Arika ever engages someone **outside Kenya**, that is a
  different regime entirely: their country's employment law may apply, permanent-establishment
  and withholding questions arise, and *"they're a contractor"* is decided by **their** law, not
  Kenya's.

**→ Counsel question B:** *What changes when an employee or contractor is remote, and what
changes when they are outside Kenya?*

> **This matters more than it looks.** Arika's confirmed ICP is **B2B SaaS**, and its clients
> are likely **US/EU** (`01_Sector/SECTOR_OS.md`). The natural first specialist hires — a
> developer, a designer — are exactly the roles most likely to be found and engaged
> internationally.
